Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value derived from an individual's weight and height. This is calculated by dividing someone’s weight in kilograms by the square of their height in meters. It is used as a tool to categorize individuals into various weight categories such as underweight and obesity. BMI is a useful and simple measure for considering weight-related health risks for someone’s vitality and will to live.

BMI ranges are used to categorize weight status and potential health risks associated with different levels of body fat. The general categories are: Underweight: BMI less than 18.5 Normal weight: BMI 18.5–24.9 Overweight: BMI 25–29.9 Obesity: BMI 30 or greater Each category can indicate different health risks. For instance, being underweight may be linked to malnutrition or other health conditions, while being overweight or obese increases the risk of diseases such as diabetes, heart disease, and certain cancers.

While BMI is a useful screening tool, it has limitations and may not accurately reflect an individual's overall health. BMI does not distinguish between muscle and fat mass, so individuals with high muscle mass (such as athletes) may have a high BMI but low body fat. Additionally, BMI does not account for the distribution of fat, which is an important factor in health risk. Other measurements, such as waist circumference and body fat percentage, can provide more comprehensive insights into an individual's health. Always consider consulting a healthcare provider for a full assessment.
